09 — FAQ

Pressure Testing FAQs

The Joint Commission and CMS expect critical rooms to be maintained within their design pressure relationships continuously, with documented verification. Most hospitals test and document at least annually, and many monitor high-risk rooms daily and verify on a quarterly or semiannual schedule. Your infection control plan and state licensing rules set the exact cadence.
ASHRAE 170 is the standard for ventilation of healthcare facilities. It sets the required pressure relationships, air change rates, and filtration for each room type. The FGI Guidelines for Design and Construction reference ASHRAE 170 and are adopted by many state health departments, so in practice the two are used together during design, construction, and ongoing compliance.
Airborne infection isolation rooms must be kept at negative pressure relative to adjacent spaces, with a minimum differential of 0.01 inches of water column per ASHRAE 170, plus a continuous visual means of verifying direction. Many facilities target a larger margin so the room stays in spec as doors open and filters load.
We can respond quickly, measure the room, and identify why it is out of spec, whether it is a terminal unit, a supply and exhaust offset, door undercut, envelope leakage, or a monitor out of calibration. We document the finding, help you correct it, and retest to confirm the room is back within design.
